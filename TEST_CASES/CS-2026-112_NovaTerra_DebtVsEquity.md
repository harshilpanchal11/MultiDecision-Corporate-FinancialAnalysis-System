## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

NovaTerra Energy Systems, Inc. is a Series C-stage grid-scale battery storage company headquartered in Austin, Texas, designing, manufacturing, and delivering lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery energy storage systems (BESS) to U.S. utilities, independent power producers (IPPs), and commercial and industrial (C&I) customers. Founded in 2018 by CEO Elena Vasquez and CFO James Okafor, the company has grown to $94.2 million in trailing twelve-month revenue as of Q1 2026—a 58% increase year-over-year—driven by accelerating demand for grid stabilization capacity as renewable penetration on U.S. power grids approaches 38%. NovaTerra currently holds a contracted backlog of $186.0 million in undelivered orders and a qualified pipeline of $412 million in active opportunities, but its 1.2 GWh/year manufacturing facility in Austin is running at 94% capacity utilization. Without a material expansion, the company will begin turning away contracts by Q4 2026, permanently damaging relationships with counterparties in a sector characterized by high switching costs and multi-decade utility procurement cycles.

To fund construction of a second manufacturing facility in Seguin, Texas—adding 1.6 GWh of annual capacity and bringing the total to 2.8 GWh/year—NovaTerra's Board of Directors must decide between two mutually exclusive $65.0 million financing paths. Option A is a $65.0 million senior secured term loan (growth capital facility) provided by Horizon Infrastructure Credit at a floating rate of SOFR + 375 bps (floor: 9.00%), structured as a 7-year instrument with a 24-month interest-only period followed by 60 months of principal and interest amortization. Option B is a $65.0 million Series D preferred equity round led by Ardent Climate Capital at a pre-money valuation of $340.0 million ($6.44 per share), representing a 9.7% step-up from NovaTerra's Series C post-money valuation of $310.0 million. Both options deliver identical gross proceeds and fund the same capital program; the decision is purely a question of capital structure, risk allocation, and long-term equity value creation.

The choice is sharpened by three intersecting constraints: (1) NovaTerra's current runway of 13.5 months (at $3.1 million/month net cash burn) requires the financing to close by Q3 2026 to maintain operating continuity and avoid a distressed raise; (2) the $340 million Series D pre-money valuation is considered by at least two existing investors to be below intrinsic value given the $186 million contracted backlog and near-term profitability inflection, making dilution at this price particularly costly; and (3) the debt option's first-lien security interest over all manufacturing assets and contract receivables would constrain NovaTerra's future asset-backed financing capacity and require lender consent for any change-of-control transaction—an important consideration given recent strategic acquisition interest from Fluence Energy and Honeywell Process Solutions.

The Board must select a financing path by June 1, 2026, to allow sufficient time for legal documentation, syndication (if debt), and investor due diligence (if equity) ahead of the construction mobilization deadline of August 15, 2026.

## COMPANY BACKGROUND

### History and Current Position

NovaTerra Energy Systems was founded in May 2018 by Elena Vasquez, former Vice President of Grid Storage at Tesla Energy, and James Okafor, former Managing Director in Morgan Stanley's Global Energy Infrastructure group. The company's founding insight was that the emerging U.S. grid storage market was being served primarily by consumer-grade lithium chemistries (NMC and NCA) that were poorly suited to the thermal and cycle-life demands of utility-scale stationary applications. NovaTerra focused exclusively on lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry—more thermally stable, offering 6,000+ cycle life at 80% depth of discharge, and with a battery management system (BMS) architecture developed in-house by CTO Dr. Raj Patel (formerly of the MIT Energy Initiative). The company achieved its first commercial deployment of a 20 MWh system with Austin Energy in October 2020 and secured its first IPP contract (a 100 MWh system with Clearway Energy) in Q1 2021.

The company raised $12.0 million in Series A funding from Energy Impact Partners in August 2020, $38.0 million in Series B from Breakthrough Energy Ventures in March 2022, and $78.0 million in Series C from BlackRock Climate Infrastructure in November 2024. Series C capital was deployed toward: expanding the Austin manufacturing facility from 400 MWh/year to 1.2 GWh/year capacity; scaling the software and O&M services organization; and hiring 94 employees to bring headcount to its current level of 312. As of Q1 2026, NovaTerra has deployed a cumulative installed base of 4.3 GWh across 43 utility, IPP, and C&I customers in 18 U.S. states, generating $28.4 million in high-margin recurring revenue from multi-year software subscriptions and operations and maintenance (O&M) service contracts layered on top of hardware project revenue.

The company's competitive differentiation rests on three pillars: its proprietary Aurora BMS platform (which reduces cell degradation by 18% versus third-party BMS alternatives over a 10-year period, per independent testing by DNV GL); vertically integrated U.S.-based manufacturing (increasingly valued following the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content incentives for ITC purposes); and a growing services organization that generates 72% gross margins and embeds NovaTerra deeply into utility operating workflows. However, NovaTerra's Austin facility is now a binding constraint on growth. At 94% utilization and with $186.0 million of contracted backlog and $412.0 million in qualified pipeline, the company is in the unusual position of having demand visibility well in excess of its capital base—but requiring $65.0 million in new capital to unlock it.

## THE DECISION

NovaTerra requires $65.0 million to fund construction of its Seguin, Texas manufacturing facility (Plant II). Both financing options provide identical gross proceeds and fund the same capital program; the expansion plan, construction timeline, and revenue ramp are identical under either option. The decision is therefore purely a capital structure question: which financing path maximizes long-term per-share equity value, preserves operational flexibility, and appropriately allocates financial risk between the company and its capital providers?

The Seguin facility, once fully operational in Q4 2027 (16 months from construction mobilization in August 2026), will add 1.6 GWh of annual LFP cell assembly and system integration capacity, bringing NovaTerra's total nameplate to 2.8 GWh/year. At projected average system revenue of $210/kWh (blended hardware, software, and O&M ACV), full utilization of Plant II represents approximately $336 million in incremental annual revenue capacity—a 3.6x multiple on the $65.0 million capital investment at full throughput.

## ADDITIONAL CONTEXT

### Board Composition and Perspectives

**Elena Vasquez, CEO and Co-Founder:** *"We are one of the fastest-growing domestic BESS manufacturers in the country, sitting on $186 million in contracted backlog we literally cannot deliver because our factory is full. This is not a speculative investment—we have purchase orders in hand. I am fundamentally reluctant to sell equity at a $340 million pre-money valuation when I believe our fair value with full manufacturing capacity is north of $600 million. Debt is the intellectually honest choice when you have demand certainty. My concern is the change-of-control clause—we have had two inbound strategic inquiries in the last six months, and I do not want a lender blocking a premium exit."*

**James Okafor, CFO and Co-Founder:** *"As a former infrastructure banker, I have structured dozens of project finance deals. The covenant package Horizon is proposing is reasonable given the asset quality—we have hard assets, ITC credits, and contracted backlog as collateral. The 2-year IO period insulates us through the construction phase, and our DSCR in Year 3 clears the 1.25x covenant by 82 basis points in the base case. The real question is what happens if we miss Year 3 EBITDA by 35–40%. That is a scenario we need to stress-test before this Board."*

**Dr. Raj Patel, CTO and Co-Founder:** *"I am technically agnostic between the two options, but I am not operationally agnostic. Plant II is a 16-month construction project with supply chain complexity I have never managed at this scale. If we hit a 6-month delay—which is not unlikely given Texas labor markets and steel tariff volatility—we will be drawing on those equity buffers heavily. I would feel more comfortable with an equity cushion during the construction window, even at the cost of dilution. Debt with covenant risk on an uncertain construction timeline keeps me up at night."*

**Marcus Thorne, Partner at Breakthrough Energy Ventures (Series B Lead):** *"We invested in NovaTerra because we believe in this team's ability to scale U.S. clean energy manufacturing. From a mission and financial perspective, I am uncomfortable locking in Series D equity at what amounts to barely a 10% step-up from our Series C post-money. NovaTerra has $186 million in backlog—show me another growth-stage hardware company with that kind of demand certainty. I think the debt option reflects the quality of this business more accurately. My LPs are patient capital; we can wait for the IPO step-up."*

**Sandra Liu, Managing Director at BlackRock Climate Infrastructure (Series C Lead):** *"BlackRock Climate Infrastructure manages $68 billion in assets. We are not indifferent to headline risk. A missed debt covenant at a clean energy company in 2026 and 2027—when this sector is under significant political scrutiny—would create reputational damage beyond what the economics suggest. Our preference is equity. A 16.1% dilution at a $340 million pre-money is a reasonable price for zero balance sheet risk through what is, objectively, an uncertain construction and ramp period. I will also note that our fund documents require us to flag any portfolio company that takes on leverage materially above 3.5x projected Year 2 EBITDA, and the $65M term loan on $18.4M Year 2 EBITDA is 3.53x—barely within my reporting threshold."*

**William Fraser, Independent Board Director (former CFO of NextEra Energy):** *"I have financed 40+ gigawatts of energy infrastructure over 25 years. Infrastructure debt on contracted assets is one of the oldest and most value-accretive tools in capital markets. NovaTerra's $186 million backlog is essentially contracted cash flow—this is not speculative technology. The 9.0% rate is high for what is fundamentally a project finance credit, but it reflects the growth-stage premium. My recommendation is debt, with an important caveat: we should negotiate a covenant step-down on the DSCR minimum if EBITDA comes in 15–20% below base case in Year 3, to prevent a technical breach from triggering acceleration before the business has the chance to recover."*

## QUESTIONS FOR ANALYSIS

### Primary Question:

Should NovaTerra Energy Systems, Inc. raise $65.0 million through a senior secured term loan at 9.00% (Option A) or through a $65.0 million Series D preferred equity round at a $340.0 million pre-money valuation (Option B) to fund its Seguin, Texas manufacturing expansion, and what is the risk-adjusted per-share equity value at Year 5 under each option across downside, base, and upside scenarios?

### Secondary Questions:

1. **Equity Value Breakeven:** Option A generates $24.26/share at Year 5 versus Option B's $21.09/share under the base case (15x EV/EBITDA). At what EBITDA multiple or at what Year 5 EBITDA level does the per-share advantage of Option A (debt) equal the per-share value of Option B (equity)? Is this breakeven achievable only in a severe downside, or does it represent a realistic probability?

2. **Covenant Stress Test:** The Year 3 DSCR covenant threshold is 1.25x; the base case projects 2.27x. If Year 3 EBITDA falls 45% below the base case ($42.8M × 55% = $23.5M), the DSCR would drop to $23.5M ÷ $18.85M = 1.25x — exactly at the covenant floor. What probability do you assign to a 45%+ EBITDA miss in Year 3, and what corrective actions (asset sales, equity cure, covenant amendment) would be available to NovaTerra prior to a technical breach?

3. **PIK Dividend Compounding Risk:** Option B includes a 6% cumulative PIK dividend on $65.0 million in Series D preferred. If NovaTerra does not achieve a qualified IPO ($500M+ market cap) within 7 years, calculate the total liquidation preference accrued by Ardent Climate Capital and the resulting dilution to common stockholders relative to the original Series D terms. At what exit valuation does the PIK accrual become economically irrelevant for founders?

4. **Construction Delay Scenario:** The CTO estimates a 15–25% probability of a 6-month construction delay at Plant II. Model the cash flow impact of a 6-month delay under both options: in Option A, does delayed production cause NovaTerra to breach the Year 2 minimum revenue covenant ($125.0M required)? In Option B, does the equity cushion provide sufficient buffer to fund the incremental $12–15M of cash burn during a 6-month extension?

5. **SOFR Rate Sensitivity:** Option A is structured as a floating-rate instrument with an 8.75% floor. If SOFR rises by 200 bps (to 7.25%) by Q3 2027, recalculate: (a) the annual interest expense in Years 2–5, (b) the revised DSCR in Years 3–5, and (c) the total additional interest cost over the 7-year term relative to the base case. Does the floating rate risk materially alter the per-share advantage of Option A?

6. **Strategic Exit Optionality:** NovaTerra has received informal acquisition interest from two strategic acquirers. Option A's change-of-control covenant requires lender consent for any transaction resulting in >35% ownership change. Option B's drag-along clause requires 75% preferred supermajority approval. Evaluate which option creates greater friction in a strategic sale process, and quantify the cost of the Option A prepayment penalty (3% in Year 1; 2% in Year 2; 1% in Year 3) if a strategic acquisition closes in Year 2 at a $650 million enterprise value.

7. **Valuation Fairness Analysis:** The Series D pre-money valuation of $340.0 million represents 3.61x NovaTerra's TTM revenue of $94.2M. Comparable grid-scale BESS companies trading publicly (Fluence Energy: 2.1x revenue; Stem Inc.: 1.4x) suggest the private market premium is substantial. Is the $340.0 million pre-money valuation justified by NovaTerra's contracted backlog and growth profile? Construct a DCF-based intrinsic value range using WACC of 12–16% and terminal FCF growth of 4–6%, and determine whether the Series D dilution represents value destruction or value-neutral capital allocation.

8. **Recommendation Reversal Conditions:** Identify the specific combination of events — EBITDA miss magnitude, SOFR rate increase, and exit timeline — under which a decision-maker who chose Option A (debt) would in hindsight have preferred Option B (equity). Conversely, identify the conditions under which a decision-maker who chose Option B would regret not preserving equity via debt at the 9.0% cost. Which set of reversal conditions is more operationally likely given NovaTerra's construction execution risk profile?
